What is the future of MMORPG's?

2024-11-30

The future of MMORPGs hinges on their ability to adapt by embracing innovation and technological advancements while preserving their core strengths of social connectivity, expansive immersive worlds, and community-driven experiences; by balancing the unbundling of genre elements into niche markets with a renewed focus on meaningful multiplayer engagement, MMORPGs can evolve to meet changing player expectations and reclaim their unique identity in the gaming landscape.

Some insights

  1. Identity Crisis of MMORPGs:

• MMORPGs have lost their unique identity by trying to appeal to mass audiences, becoming jack-of-all-trades that fail to excel in any single aspect.

• This broad appeal design dilutes what makes MMORPGs special, leading to a lack of distinctiveness in the genre.

  1. Unbundling of MMORPG Elements into Niche Genres:

• Core features of MMORPGs—like sociality, combat, and economy—are being split into specialized genres (e.g., MOBAs, survival games), causing fragmentation.

• This splintering leads to traditional MMORPGs losing their comprehensive appeal.

  1. Core Appeal is Community and Social Interaction:

• The unique strength of MMORPGs lies in their ability to create persistent, shared worlds fostering social interaction and collaborative experiences.

• Dilution of these social elements diminishes the genre’s distinctiveness.

  1. Players Prefer Focused Experiences Over “Everything Box” MMOs:

• The rise of specialized games reflects a player preference for games that focus on specific aspects rather than trying to encompass all elements.

• This shift contributes to the decline of traditional, all-encompassing MMORPGs.

  1. Optimism for the Future of MMORPGs Through Innovation:

• Despite challenges, there is optimism that MMORPGs can innovate and evolve, embracing new technologies like VR and AR.

• Indie projects and technological advancements could lead to a resurgence of the genre.

  1. Nostalgia for the Golden Age and Decline of Traditional MMOs:

• Players reminisce about the genre’s golden age, longing for immersive, community-driven worlds.

• Many traditional MMOs have failed or stagnated, leaving a gap in the market.

  1. MMORPGs Are Evolving, Not Dying:

• The genre is diversifying rather than disappearing, adapting to new market demands and player expectations.

• There’s room for both traditional MMORPGs and innovative, modern multiplayer experiences.

  1. Players’ Role in the Evolution and Changing Habits:

• Players have shifted towards efficiency and convenience, focusing on min-maxing rather than exploration and community building.

• This change in player behavior influences how MMORPGs are designed and perceived.

  1. Need for MMORPGs to Adapt by Reducing Barriers and Focusing on Strengths:

• To remain relevant, MMORPGs must streamline designs, reduce grind, and emphasize their strengths in community and large-scale collaboration.

• Adapting to modern design philosophies can help attract new players.

  1. Challenges from Other Genres Filling Traditional MMO Roles:

• Genres like co-op games, battle royales, and survival games are fulfilling social and gameplay needs once unique to MMORPGs.

• This competition pressures MMORPGs to differentiate themselves.

  1. Negativity and Toxicity in the MMO Community:

• Increasing negativity in communities affects player experience and can demotivate developers.

• Constructive criticism is necessary, but excessive toxicity hinders the genre’s growth.

  1. Shift to Blockbuster MMOs and Decline of Mid-Budget Projects:

• The success of games like World of Warcraft led to an industry focus on high-budget MMOs, neglecting mid-budget games.

• This shift has reduced diversity and increased financial risks in the genre.

  1. Hope for Industry Change and Return to Sustainable Models:

• There’s potential for the industry to pivot back to more sustainable, innovative models, possibly led by veteran developers.

• Recognizing the value of mid-budget MMOs could revitalize the market.

  1. Indie MMOs and Technological Advances as Solutions:

• Indie studios and new technologies (e.g., SpatialOS) offer opportunities to create expansive MMOs without massive budgets.

• These could address current limitations and bring fresh ideas to the genre.

  1. Challenges of Pleasing a Diverse Player Base:

• Diverse expectations and preferences make it difficult for developers to create universally appealing MMORPGs.

• Balancing depth, accessibility, and innovation is a complex task for the future of the genre.

The question of whether rebundling the aspects of MMORPGs would be effective, or if unbundling into specialized genres is the right path, is a complex one that hinges on player preferences, market trends, and the unique strengths of the MMORPG genre.

Arguments for Unbundling:

  1. Focused Experiences: Unbundling allows games to specialize in particular aspects of gameplay, providing more refined and polished experiences. For example, MOBAs like League of Legends focus intensely on competitive multiplayer combat, while RPGs like Skyrim offer deep single-player narratives.

  2. Player Preferences: Many players have shown a preference for games that excel in specific areas rather than attempting to be a comprehensive “everything box.” This specialization caters to varied tastes and allows players to choose games that align closely with their interests.

  3. Market Trends: The gaming industry has seen success in genres that have emerged from the unbundling of MMORPG elements. Survival games, battle royales, and co-op experiences have attracted large player bases by honing in on particular gameplay mechanics.

  4. Accessibility: Specialized games often have lower barriers to entry, both in terms of time commitment and complexity. This accessibility can attract a broader audience who might be deterred by the depth and scale of traditional MMORPGs.

Arguments for Rebundling:

  1. Unique Strengths of MMORPGs: The core appeal of MMORPGs lies in their ability to create expansive, persistent worlds that foster social interaction and community building. Rebundling these aspects could recapture the immersive experiences that players reminisce about from the genre’s golden age.

  2. Comprehensive Experiences: MMORPGs offer a unique blend of storytelling, exploration, combat, and social dynamics that can’t be fully replicated in specialized games. Rebundling could provide players with a rich, multifaceted experience.

  3. Community and Social Interaction: The shared experiences and long-term relationships formed in MMORPGs are a significant draw for many players. Rebundling could strengthen these social bonds by bringing all elements back into a unified platform.

  4. Differentiation: In a market saturated with specialized games, a well-executed, comprehensive MMORPG could stand out by offering something that no other genre does—a fully integrated world where all aspects of gameplay coexist.

Challenges to Rebundling:

  1. Development Costs and Time: Creating a comprehensive MMORPG is resource-intensive, requiring significant investment and long development cycles. This poses a financial risk that many studios are reluctant to take.

  2. Changing Player Habits: Modern players often have less time and may prefer games that offer quick, focused experiences. Rebundling might not align with the lifestyles of today’s broader gaming audience.

  3. Competition from Specialized Genres: With other genres effectively catering to specific player needs, a rebundled MMORPG would need to compete on multiple fronts, excelling in all areas to attract and retain players.

  4. Risk of Dilution: Without careful design, attempting to include all aspects of gameplay could result in a game that is spread too thin, failing to excel in any particular area and not meeting the high expectations of players.

Is Unbundling the Right Choice?

Unbundling has allowed for innovation and the creation of new genres that provide focused enjoyment. It caters to specific interests and adapts to modern gaming trends. However, it has also led to the fragmentation of the MMORPG player base and a dilution of the genre’s unique identity.

Potential for a Middle Ground:

Rather than viewing rebundling and unbundling as mutually exclusive, there may be a middle path:

  1. Modular Design: MMORPGs could adopt a modular approach, allowing players to engage deeply with the aspects they enjoy while still existing within a unified world. This could involve optional content that caters to different playstyles without overwhelming players.

  2. Innovation Within the Genre: Embracing new technologies like VR, AR, or cloud gaming could reinvigorate the MMORPG experience, offering fresh ways to interact with expansive worlds.

  3. Focus on Core Strengths: By honing in on what makes MMORPGs special—social connectivity, persistent worlds, and community-driven narratives—developers can create compelling experiences without necessarily trying to include every possible gameplay element.

  4. Flexible Monetization Models: Moving away from predatory monetization and adopting fair, player-friendly business models could attract and retain players who are wary of traditional MMORPG time and financial commitments.

Conclusion:

Rebundling the aspects of MMORPGs could work if done thoughtfully, addressing modern players’ preferences while preserving the genre’s core appeal. It requires balancing depth with accessibility and innovation with tradition. Unbundling has its merits, offering specialized experiences that cater to specific interests. However, there’s still a place for comprehensive MMORPGs that provide rich, interconnected worlds.

The future of MMORPGs may not lie strictly in rebundling or unbundling but in evolving to combine the best of both approaches. By focusing on their unique strengths and adapting to changing market dynamics, MMORPGs can continue to thrive and offer unparalleled experiences in the gaming landscape.

Key Points Summary

  1. Instrumental vs. Free Play:

• Instrumental play is goal-oriented, optimizing strategies for efficiency, while free play focuses on enjoyment without specific objectives.

• Neither form exists in pure isolation; they coexist in tension within games like World of Warcraft (WoW).

  1. Evolution of Social Practices:

• Players shape what is considered “good” play through consensus, not inherent game mechanics.

• Social codes define legitimacy in play, often making “bad” gameplay socially unacceptable.

  1. Role of Paratext:

• Paratexts (guides, videos, add-ons) influence how players perceive and engage with WoW, embedding instrumental values.

• Developers and players co-create the game, with paratexts pushing instrumental practices into the mainstream.

  1. Impact of AddOns:

• AddOns enable optimization but contribute to homogenization of playstyles.

• They alter gameplay, from making encounters easier to influencing raid strategies, blurring lines between human and software contributions.

  1. Cultural and Social Dynamics:

• WoW’s playerbase is fragmented into idiocultures (e.g., raiders vs. roleplayers), each with distinct values and norms.

• Success is socially defined, with expectations shaped by top-tier guilds and paratext dissemination.

  1. Modern Challenges in WoW:

• Instrumental play often erodes the immersive and exploratory aspects of the game.

• Practices like theorycrafting and optimization have shifted player focus to achieving efficiency at the expense of aesthetic and narrative engagement.

  1. Classic WoW Paradox:

• Intended as a nostalgic return to simpler gameplay, WoW Classic has instead become dominated by speedrunning and optimization.

• Instrumental practices imported from modern WoW persist, diluting the intended experience.

  1. Blizzard’s Influence:

• Blizzard shapes WoW through updates and tools but cannot entirely control how players engage with the game.

• The interplay of developer intent and player practices creates a unique but chaotic gaming ecosystem.

  1. Philosophical Reflections:

• Virtual worlds are extensions of real-life human behavior and culture, not isolated from reality.

• The meaning of WoW emerges from player interaction, making it a compelling study of collaborative human engagement.

  1. The Messy Beauty of WoW:

• Despite its flaws, WoW’s emergent culture reflects the complexity and contradictions of humanity.

• The game’s value lies in its ability to create meaningful, shared experiences, even amid the dominance of instrumental play.

Key Points: Why World of Warcraft is Considered Toxic

  1. Toxic Communities in Gaming:

• Games like League of Legends, Call of Duty, and Counter-Strike are often criticized for their hostile and competitive player bases.

• World of Warcraft (WoW) also has a reputation for toxicity due to its long history and evolving community dynamics.

  1. Types of Play:

• Free Play: Early WoW gameplay emphasized exploration, creativity, and a relaxed community dynamic.

• Instrumental Play: Over time, the focus shifted to achieving specific goals (e.g., gear, rankings, speed runs), introducing pressure, elitism, and a competitive atmosphere.

• The transition from free play to instrumental play leads to increased tension, as enjoyment becomes tied to performance rather than the experience.

  1. Game Design’s Role in Toxicity:

• Developers often cater to instrumental play by introducing systems that highlight performance metrics (e.g., gear scores, raid completions).

• This encourages min-maxing, metagaming, and exclusion of players who don’t meet optimal standards.

  1. Player Behavior:

• Competitive human nature drives the need to “win” or outperform others.

• Elitism and exclusion arise as players prioritize efficiency over camaraderie.

  1. Long-Term Investment:

• WoW players often invest heavily in time, money, and emotion over years or even decades.

• This creates a deeply personal attachment to the game, making criticism or changes to the game feel like personal attacks.

  1. Community Division:

• The community is split between:

• Toxic Positivity: Fans who defend the game vehemently.

• Toxic Negativity: Critics who disdain its modern state or Blizzard’s controversies.

• New players may feel alienated or overwhelmed by these polarized dynamics.

  1. Blizzard’s Role:

• Blizzard’s controversies, including poor expansion releases, layoffs, and lawsuits, have fueled criticism.

• Some criticism is valid, while other negativity stems from bandwagoning or bad faith.

  1. Hyper-Optimization’s Impact:

• Features like damage meters and parsing create environments where players are judged strictly on performance.

• In some games (e.g., Final Fantasy XIV), measures are taken to limit public display of performance metrics, but WoW embraces them.

  1. MMO-Specific Challenges:

• MMOs like WoW foster intense investment, as they require long-term financial and time commitments.

• The persistent, shared world increases emotional attachment, making both positive and negative experiences more intense.

  1. Human Nature:

• Toxicity in WoW and other games is more a reflection of human behavior than game design.

• Competitive instincts and overinvestment in passions lead to charged dynamics and unwelcoming environments.

  1. Final Thoughts:

• Toxicity in WoW stems from a mix of competitive gameplay, overinvestment, and community polarization.

• The problem isn’t unique to WoW—it reflects broader issues in how people engage with long-term, passion-driven entertainment.

Key Points: Nostalgia and MMO Communities

  1. Understanding Nostalgia:

• Nostalgia evokes fond memories of the past, such as childhood games, songs, or experiences.

• In gaming, nostalgia often fuels interest in classic titles like World of Warcraft (WoW) and RuneScape.

  1. Gatekeeping Nostalgia:

• Nostalgia is sometimes dismissed in gaming communities, particularly with WoW Vanilla and Old School RuneScape.

• Critics argue these games are only popular because of nostalgia, disregarding their original appeal and ongoing success.

  1. Popularity of Nostalgic Games:

• Nostalgia may draw players initially, but long-term popularity requires intrinsic quality and ongoing updates.

• WoW Classic and Old School RuneScape have proven successful, refuting claims they only appeal due to nostalgia.

  1. Tribalism in MMOs:

• MMOs demand significant investment (time, money, emotion), creating defensive player bases.

• Competition between MMOs fosters tribalism, where players aggressively defend their favorite games and dismiss others.

  1. The Role of Community:

• Nostalgia often builds a strong sense of community, with players creating their own challenges and experiences.

• Examples include hardcore modes in WoW Classic and unique player-driven challenges in Old School RuneScape.

  1. Appeal of Simplicity:

• Many players enjoy the charm and simplicity of older game designs, which modern games sometimes lack.

• Retro-style games, whether nostalgic or newly developed, remain highly popular (e.g., Stardew Valley, Undertale).

  1. Nostalgia as a Design Philosophy:

• Nostalgia-driven games cater to players’ desire for familiar, enjoyable experiences.

• Updates and creative directions can sustain interest beyond initial nostalgic appeal.

  1. Updates and Community Feedback:

• Player-driven content and updates, as seen in Old School RuneScape, keep nostalgic games relevant.

• Polling players for input on updates fosters engagement and ensures the game evolves in line with community preferences.

  1. Lessons from Nostalgia:

• Nostalgia highlights the lasting appeal of well-designed games and the value of respecting players’ memories.

• Developers can use nostalgia to inspire modern design while avoiding exploitative cash grabs.

  1. Conclusion: Embrace Nostalgia:

• Nostalgia isn’t inherently negative; it’s a legitimate reason to enjoy games.

• Players should be free to enjoy games for any reason, whether nostalgic or not, without gatekeeping or dismissive criticism.

Key Points: Why MMOs Are Perceived as Less Social

  1. Core Dependency of MMOs:

• MMOs are unique because their gameplay is inherently tied to the presence of other players.

• The value of achievements, rare items, and progression is relative to others; without a community, much of the appeal fades.

  1. Shift in Social Dynamics:

• Early MMOs relied on a “weak tie interdependence” model, where strangers were crucial for success (e.g., grouping for dungeons, trading items, or comparing performance).

• This dependency encouraged collaboration, interaction, and treating others as valuable assets.

  1. Evolving Environment:

• Knowledge Accessibility:

• Database websites, data mining, and guide culture solve content before release, reducing the need for player-to-player interaction.

• Communication Shifts:

• Platforms like Discord and Twitch replace in-game chat, realm forums, and guild discussions.

• Stagnant Player Base:

• MMOs attract fewer new players and retain aging, familiar players who no longer rely on others as they once did.

  1. Impact of Familiarity:

• Familiarity diminishes the challenge and dependency that once fostered social ties.

• Players no longer need to ask questions or collaborate, reducing opportunities for meaningful interactions.

  1. Design Changes in Modern MMOs:

• Many MMOs now cater to instant gratification and reduced attention spans:

• Automated systems (e.g., matchmaking, raid finders) prioritize efficiency over social bonding.

• Solo-friendly content minimizes reliance on others for success.

• These changes aim to attract a newer generation but sacrifice the social foundations of the genre.

  1. Diminished Uniqueness of MMOs:

• Features once exclusive to MMOs (e.g., talent systems, persistent worlds, loot mechanics) have been adopted by other genres, reducing the genre’s distinct appeal.

• MMOs were pioneers of social gaming, but the rise of mainstream social media has overshadowed their unique community-driven experiences.

  1. Player Retention and Sunk Cost Fallacy:

• Long-term players hesitate to leave their MMOs due to the time and money invested, reinforcing a stagnant player base.

• New MMOs struggle to attract this audience, leading to fewer opportunities for fresh social dynamics.

  1. Designing for Convenience Over Community:

• Developers aim to streamline gameplay for modern players, focusing on accessibility and ease:

• Shared mob tagging, reduced group requirements, and simplified progression systems decrease the need for cooperation.

• These design choices make games more antisocial by removing dependencies.

  1. Antisocial Social Game:

• MMOs, once the most social genre, have evolved into some of the least social multiplayer experiences.

• The erosion of dependence, combined with changing player habits and design philosophies, has created a genre that feels isolated despite being inherently social.

Key Talking Points: “Will We Ever See Another Great MMO?”

  1. MMOs as a Unique Genre:

• Unlike other game genres, MMOs have few major successes and a highly monopolistic landscape.

• Most MMOs fail to sustain their initial hype, settling into niche audiences or shutting down.

  1. Volatile Life Cycles:

• Many MMOs have massive launches fueled by hype but fail to meet expectations, leading to rapid declines.

• Few exceptions like Final Fantasy XIV demonstrate long-term growth after initial failure.

  1. Challenges of MMO Development:

• MMOs are resource-intensive, requiring significant funding, time, and risk.

• Development is dominated by AAA studios, but AAA games have increasingly lost consumer trust.

  1. Role of Hype and Expectations:

• Pre-release hype often sets unrealistic expectations, leading to inevitable disappointment.

• Games like New World and Lost Ark failed to sustain player interest despite strong launches.

  1. Monetization Issues:

• Modern MMOs rely heavily on microtransactions and cash shops, which can alienate players.

• A focus on monetization over innovation erodes game quality and player trust.

  1. Impact of Social Media and Changing Player Bases:

• The rise of social media has shifted player interaction and engagement away from in-game activities.

• The MMO player base is aging, while younger audiences gravitate toward faster-paced games like Warzone and Fortnite.

  1. Barriers for New MMOs:

• Existing MMOs retain players through sunk cost fallacies and established communities.

• New MMOs struggle to attract and retain players in a highly competitive space dominated by established titles.

  1. Potential for Innovation:

• To succeed, future MMOs must innovate, moving away from traditional “kill 20 goblins” quests and linear progression.

• Sandbox MMOs and community-driven content offer untapped potential for engaging players.

  1. Role of Leveling and Endgame Design:

• Modern MMOs often neglect leveling in favor of endgame content, missing opportunities to hook players early.

• A more immersive, extended leveling experience could redefine player engagement.

  1. The Importance of Community:

• Successful MMOs foster dedicated communities that persist even when official servers shut down.

• Player-made challenges, unique game modes, and sandbox features could help keep communities active and engaged.

  1. Hope for the Future:

• While the MMO market is currently stagnant, technological advancements and innovative design could lead to a breakthrough.

• Developers must prioritize long-term player retention through quality game design over quick monetization schemes.

  1. Discussion on WoW’s Success:

• World of Warcraft was a product of its time, thriving during the early days of social media and online communities.

• Its success redefined the genre, but it also created a monopolistic environment that stifled innovation.

  1. Outlook for the Next MMO Revolution:

• The next major MMO needs to break free from existing tropes and address modern player expectations.

• Innovation in design and engagement will be critical, but the path to success remains uncertain.

Key Talking Points: “The Magic of MMORPGs and Why It’s Fading”

  1. The “Magic” of MMORPGs:

• The magic is rooted in the unknown, including unfamiliar systems, characters, and a sense of adventure shared with others.

• This feeling of wonder, akin to memorable moments in other iconic games, fades once familiarity sets in.

  1. Core Design of MMORPGs:

• MMOs operate on a carrot and treadmill system:

• The carrot represents rewards like gear, levels, or status.

• The treadmill represents the grind to achieve them.

• Skilled developers create treadmills that are fun and rewarding; exceptional developers disguise them entirely.

  1. Why the Magic Fades:

• Players quickly exhaust the “unknown” as they learn the mechanics and optimize their gameplay.

• The dopamine-driven experience diminishes over time, requiring greater effort to sustain the same level of enjoyment.

  1. Impact of Modern Gaming Culture:

• The availability of information (e.g., guides, streams, data mining) means players often “solve” MMOs before they are even released.

• Social media and external resources encourage players to optimize, sometimes at the expense of enjoyment.

• Those who intentionally avoid optimization to preserve the magic risk being ostracized by others for falling behind.

  1. Challenges for MMO Developers:

• MMOs are inherently multiplayer, making player experiences highly variable and hard to control.

• Developers face pressure to design content that keeps players engaged while managing costs and risks.

• Systems meant to encourage fun can be exploited, as players prioritize efficiency over experience (e.g., Tol Barad in World of Warcraft).

  1. The Role of World of Warcraft:

• WoW’s success in 2004 set the standard for MMORPGs with its “theme park” approach: linear leveling, handcrafted content, and predictable endgame loops.

• This formula became the industry standard, stifling innovation and experimentation.

  1. The Problem of Iteration Over Innovation:

• Developers focus on safe, proven systems rather than risky, innovative ideas.

• This stagnation prevents MMOs from recapturing the magic of discovery and adventure.

  1. Modern Player Expectations:

• Today’s players are more knowledgeable and demand efficiency, which can undermine the immersive, adventurous aspects of MMOs.

• The magic is harder to capture when players are aware of and resistant to the treadmill beneath them.

  1. Barriers to Change:

• High development costs and unpredictable player behavior discourage bold experimentation.

• Iteration ensures financial viability but perpetuates sameness, eroding the genre’s appeal.

  1. The Future of MMORPGs:

• Without significant innovation, MMOs risk remaining stagnant.

• Reclaiming the magic requires breaking from established formulas and embracing the unknown, though the financial risks make this unlikely.

  1. Conclusion:

• The MMORPG genre thrives on the unknown, but the industry’s reliance on safe design choices has diminished its sense of wonder.

• The only way forward is to innovate and take risks, even if the path to recapturing the magic remains uncertain.
